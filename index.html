Introduction to Discourse Analysis

Discourse analysis is the study of language in use, whether in the form of text or talk. It goes beyond the sentence level to understand how language is used in real-life contexts to achieve social purposes.

Rather than focusing on the formal structure of language, discourse analysis examines how people use language to do things, to be certain kinds of people, and to make sense of the world and their experiences in it.

Big 'D' vs. Little 'd' Discourse

James Paul Gee distinguishes between "big D" Discourse and "little d" discourse. "Little d" discourse refers to language-in-use, or the actual instances of communication. "Big D" Discourse encompasses more than just language; it includes ways of behaving, interacting, valuing, thinking, believing, and speaking that are accepted as instantiations of particular identities by specific groups.

Primary Discourse

Primary Discourse refers to the ways of using language, thinking, acting, interacting, and valuing that people learn early in life through their interactions with family and close community members. It forms the foundation of our identity and our initial worldview.

Your Notes

Secondary Discourse

Secondary Discourses are those we develop through participation in institutions beyond the family, such as schools, workplaces, religious organisations, and community groups. These discourses often require more explicit teaching and learning than Primary Discourses.

Social Languages

Social languages are styles of language used in different social contexts. We all use different social languages in different situations—the way we speak to friends differs from how we speak in a job interview or academic setting.

Recognition and Identity

Identity Construction

Discourse analysis helps in understanding how individuals construct their identities through language and communication. Our identities are not fixed entities but are continually shaped and reshaped through discursive practices. By analysing how people use language in various contexts, we can uncover the ways in which they position themselves and are positioned by others.

Social Recognition

Discourse analysis explores how people use language to gain recognition and acceptance within specific social groups. Through linguistic choices, individuals signal their belonging to particular communities and seek validation from others. This process of recognition is crucial for social integration and the development of a sense of self within a community.

Power Dynamics

Discourse analysis examines how language can reinforce or challenge power dynamics, influencing how identities are perceived and recognised. Language is not neutral; it reflects and reproduces social structures and hierarchies. By critically analysing discourse, we can uncover how certain identities are privileged while others are marginalised, and how these power relations are maintained or contested through language.

Tools of Inquiry in Discourse Analysis

Discourse analysis is not a single method, but a collection of tools and approaches for studying language in use. Gee identifies four key "tools of inquiry" that are particularly useful for a sociocultural approach to discourse analysis:

  • Social Languages: As we've discussed, these are the different styles of language that we use in different contexts. Analyzing social languages involves looking at the specific vocabulary, grammar, and interactional styles that are associated with a particular social group or setting.
  • Discourses: This involves looking at the bigger picture, the "identity kits" that people use to enact different social identities. Analyzing Discourses involves looking at how people use language, as well as other semiotic resources like clothing, gestures, and objects, to get recognized as a certain kind of person.
  • Intertextuality: This refers to the way that texts are always in conversation with other texts. Analyzing intertextuality involves looking at how a particular text borrows from, refers to, or alludes to other texts. This can reveal how a text is positioned within a larger conversation or debate.
  • Conversations: This refers to the ongoing public conversations that are happening in a society about a particular topic. Analyzing conversations involves looking at how a particular text contributes to, or is shaped by, these larger social conversations.

Conducting a Discourse Analysis Project

A discourse analysis project typically involves the following steps:

  1. Formulating a research question: What do you want to find out about language in use? Your research question should be specific and focused.
  2. Collecting data: This might involve recording conversations, collecting written texts, or taking field notes.
  3. Transcribing data: If you are working with spoken data, you will need to transcribe it. This involves writing down not just the words that are spoken, but also other relevant information like pauses, intonation, and gestures.
  4. Analyzing data: This is the heart of the project. It involves using the tools of discourse analysis to identify patterns and themes in your data.
  5. Interpreting data: What do your findings mean? How do they help you to answer your research question?

Research Questions in Discourse Analysis

Discourse analysis can be used to address a wide range of research questions, such as:

  • How do doctors and patients communicate with each other?
  • How do politicians use language to persuade voters?
  • How do students from different cultural backgrounds participate in classroom discussions?
  • How is gender represented in advertising?
